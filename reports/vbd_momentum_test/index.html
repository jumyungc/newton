See-saw & Scale: Granular vs Monolithic Loads

Two pedagogical experiments to test whether 1 big cube and 64 small cubes packed into the same shape with the same total mass behave the same way under gravity. Goal: probe momentum / mass conservation across discretization for XPBD, Soft VBD, and Hard VBD.

Loads compared1 big · 64 small
Total mass each side1000 kg
External shape1 m cube
SolversXPBD · Soft / Hard VBD

What this report is

Newton's pyramid stress test compares solvers on a single discretization (cubes of fixed size). This report asks the complementary question: given two loads with the same total mass and the same external shape but different cube-count discretization, does each solver treat them as gravitationally equivalent?

Two experiments answer that:

  • See-saw — a balance beam (light rigid body on a revolute joint). Place one load on each side. If the gravitational torques cancel, the beam stays horizontal. If a solver biases one side, the beam tips. This is also a sensitive symmetry test: mirror-image loads should produce mirror-image responses.
  • Static scale — both loads sitting side by side on the same ground plane. Each load's center-of-mass height after settling reveals whether the solver maintains the expected 0.5 m equilibrium (= half-extent of the bounding cube).

Mass / shape recipe (matched exactly):

  • Big cube: half-extent 0.5 m → side 1 m → volume 1 m³ → density 1000 kg/m³ → 1000 kg.
  • 64 small cubes (4×4×4): half-extent 0.125 m → side 0.25 m → 64 × 0.015625 m³ × 1000 kg/m³ = 1000 kg total, packed tight (centers 0.2502 m apart) into a 1 m cube footprint.

Precondition — which solvers can hold an NxNxN stack on a plane?

Before any see-saw or scale test, the granular load must be self-supporting under gravity. Tight-packed cubic grids are surprisingly hard: contacts are simultaneous, in tight clusters, and small numerical errors compound across layers. Sweep over N at ss=10, mu=0.6, density=1000, with each solver's recommended Track-B configuration:

Two surprises in the precondition test:

  • XPBD with newton defaults under-resolves tight cube grids at any N ≥ 3, dropping 8–16% of stack CoM as cubes shift sideways. Fixable by raising iter from 2 to 8 (then CoM drop falls to ~1%), at 4× the solver cost.
  • Soft VBD cannot hold a 5×5×5 stack — penalty-only contact resolution can't propagate constraints transitively through a 5-deep grid. Even at ss=30, iter=4, ke=1e6 (120 inner iterations per frame), the stack still drops 40% of CoM. The fix is to use Hard VBD (AVBD Lagrangian propagates constraints across layers) or step down to N=4 (64 cubes) which all three solvers handle.

Decision: the see-saw and scale experiments below all use N=4 (64 cubes) and per-solver tuned configs (XPBD: iter=8; Soft & Hard VBD: iter=4, ke=5e5) so all three solvers participate cleanly in the comparison.

Experiment 1 — See-saw

A 6 m × 1.5 m × 0.2 m beam (180 kg) is mounted on a revolute joint about its Y-axis at the scene origin. Two loads are placed symmetrically on top at x = ±2 m. The see-saw is an unstable equilibrium: any imbalance grows because the off-pivot load shifts further out as the beam tips. So the test is sensitive to small per-step asymmetries.

1A — Control test: mirror loads on both sides (must stay balanced)

Same load on each side. By symmetry, the beam should not tip — every gravitational torque on the left has a mirror on the right. Any tipping indicates the solver isn't preserving the mirror symmetry of the inputs.

Solverbig-big (1 big cube each side)small-small (64-cube stack each side)
XPBD ss=10, iter=80.000° peak (perfect)0.078° peak (essentially perfect)
Soft VBD ss=10, iter=4, ke=5e50.002° peak (perfect)35.4° peak (tips violently)
Hard VBD ss=10, iter=4, ke=5e50.000° peak (perfect)33.6° peak (tips violently)

VBD breaks mirror symmetry under granular loads. Two identical 64-cube stacks on opposite sides of the pivot should produce identical responses. They don't, in either VBD mode. The cause is in the solver, not the scene: parallel Gauss-Seidel iterates bodies in color-group order, and the assigned colors of the +x stack differ from the −x stack's. Each stack therefore settles to a slightly different micro-equilibrium, and that tiny asymmetry triggers the unstable see-saw to tip. With 1 big cube on each side (no internal contacts), VBD handles it perfectly — confirming the issue is intra-stack coloring asymmetry, not joint dynamics.

1B — Main test: 1 big cube vs 64 small cubes (same mass, same shape)

If mass is correctly conserved across discretization, the gravitational torque from 64 small cubes (1000 kg total, CoM at x=+2 m) equals the torque from 1 big cube (1000 kg, CoM at x=-2 m). The see-saw should remain balanced.

1C — Where does the asymmetry come from? (small-small control videos)

The granular-stack tipping is not specific to the 1-cube vs 64-cube comparison. The same tipping happens when both sides carry identical 64-cube stacks. That's the symmetry-breakdown evidence: VBD's parallel Gauss-Seidel coloring assigns colors based on body indices, and the +x and −x stacks get non-mirror-symmetric color orderings. Each stack finds a slightly different micro-equilibrium; the unstable see-saw amplifies the difference.

Experiment 2 — Static scale (two loads side-by-side)

Both loads sit on the same flat ground plane, 5 m apart. After settling, each load's center-of-mass height should be at z = 0.5 m (the half-extent of the bounding 1 m cube). A solver that loses mass — or compresses the granular stack — will show a lower CoM for the small-cube load than for the big cube.

All three solvers correctly preserve total mass under gravity. Hard VBD is the tightest at 1.3 mm difference; Soft VBD is 7 mm; XPBD's small-cube load drops 33 mm because the grid cubes spread laterally (not because mass is lost — the CoM is still mostly at the right height; the drop is from the corner cubes sliding outward).

What the two experiments together say

  • Mass / gravity IS correctly preserved across discretization for all three solvers. The static-scale comparison shows that 1000 kg as 1 big cube and 1000 kg as 64 small cubes both settle to within a few mm of the same CoM height.
  • XPBD additionally preserves mirror symmetry of momenta. The see-saw stays balanced (≤ 0.08° tilt) whether the loads are mirror-image granular stacks or one-big-vs-many-small. This is the property the see-saw was meant to probe; XPBD passes cleanly.
  • VBD breaks mirror symmetry under granular loads. Two identical 64-cube stacks on opposite sides of a see-saw don't behave identically — the beam tips by 33–35° in both Soft and Hard VBD modes. The root cause is in the solver: parallel Gauss-Seidel iterates bodies in color-group order, and color-group assignment isn't mirror-symmetric across the scene's reflection plane. Each stack reaches a slightly different micro-equilibrium; the unstable see-saw amplifies the difference into a tipping cascade.
  • The 1-vs-64 tipping in VBD is therefore not a mass-conservation failure — it's the same Gauss-Seidel asymmetry, made worse by the granular side's internal contact compliance.
  • For practical use: Hard VBD on a stable platform (or a one-sided static scene) gives the tightest mass behavior of the three solvers. But for any test that depends on mirror-symmetric behavior under granular loads (closely-matched stacks, perfectly-aligned pile collapse, balance-scale apps), XPBD is the correct choice; VBD's coloring isn't symmetric enough.
