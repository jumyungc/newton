The premise: local VBD hits a global-residual wall on long cables

VBD (Vertex Block Descent) updates each body using only its immediate neighbors, so constraint corrections propagate along a chain at one body per iteration. On an N-body cable with K iterations, the residual scales as O(K/N). Increasing K beyond a small multiple of N does not help because the local Gauss-Seidel sweep stalls on the low-frequency modes of the chain.

Our first witness is long_cantilever_1024_superbend500: 1024 bodies, one end fixed, stretch stiffness 1×10⁵, bend stiffness 500× nominal (2500). If the solver were global, the chain would hold essentially straight. Local VBD leaves it bent at ~4.4° per joint — at any iteration count.

Theory confirmed. Fully N-invariant residuals. VBD's wall at bend_max = 0.076 rad is a fixed neighborhood accuracy. Global Thomas hits the discretization floor at 0.00414 rad. Both invariants of N. The 18.4× residual ratio holds across N = 1024, 2048, 4096. VBD step time is constant (~30 ms) — coloring-bottlenecked, not FLOP-bounded. Method S + Thomas-bend scales linearly with N (57 → 112 → 234 ms) — exactly as expected for O(N) sequential Thomas. At N = 4096 the step-time ratio narrows to 7.6× VBD@K=80, but the residual advantage stays at 18.4×.

Methodology notes

All runs on the same hardware (single NVIDIA GPU), Warp backend, identical scene setup, identical dt and frame budget. Every method tuned to its honest best within one config across scenes — no per-scene parameter tweaks. Where a metric appears large in magnitude but small in effect (e.g., energy drift with near-zero denominator) we describe the physical interpretation, not just the numeric value.
