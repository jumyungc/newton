Headline — Method R v2 + XPBD bend

One solver, four quadrants: stiff bend / soft bend × with-contact / without-contact. Same algorithm, same hyper-parameters — only the scene's bend_ke changes. Source: bench/global_cable/method_a_warp.py → substep_r(apply_bend=True).

Method R v2 composes three positional-XPBD pieces (NOTES §47):

  • Distance — direction-aware block-tridiag Thomas (from Method N v1): one global O(N) sweep per cable. Locked rotation; child position only.
  • Angular impulse — every distance multiplier λ also drives a body-frame torque proportional to r × n; trace-isotropic Schur term ‖r‖² · trace(I⁻¹)/3 is added to the scalar tridiag diagonal so the global solve stays scalar.
  • Bend — per-pair XPBD projection of C = axis_angle(q_p⁻¹·q_c·q_rest⁻¹), in parent body frame then rotated to world via R_p. Critical: missing this rotate-out broke piles where parents have mixed orientations.

Cost: Method R per iter ≈ 4.4× VBD per iter on chains, but K=20 is enough to match VBD@400 quality on the 1024-segment chain (Method R wall-clock ≈ 5× faster than VBD@400 at the same residual; ≈ comparable wall-clock to VBD@88). Method S — the new bestest (NEW, 2026-06-17): Method R + translation-only SOR (successive over-relaxation) between outer iters. Snapshot → distance + bend → SOR-blend with ω = 1.5 → contact projection. Zero step-time cost (kernel is one transform load + write per body).

Universal omega = 1.5: wins on all 5 XL pile bend variants (stiff → rope), all 3 long-chain cantilevers (256/512/1024 seg), and the short chains. Tested ω = 1.9 first and found it regressed on very-soft bend (+20 % stretch on bend_ke = 0.005); ω = 1.5 wins everywhere. No per-scene tuning, no method dispatch.

Critical ordering insight: SOR must be applied BEFORE the contact projection — putting it after caused 3.5× higher bend and 4× higher vmax (contact-corrected positions get extrapolated back into penetration). The reorder lets contacts have the last word per iter, killing the oscillation. SOR-before-contacts is the canonical form.

Universal omega search: ω = 1.5 was found by sweeping ω ∈ {1.0, 1.1, …, 1.9, 1.95, 1.99} on a short chain (max gain at ω → 2), then validating on the XL bend sweep where ω = 1.9 broke down at very-soft bend. ω = 1.5 is the universal sweet spot — captures most of the gain on stiff cases and avoids over-correction on soft cases.

Thomas-bend extension (NEW, 2026-06-18) — fixes bend saturation on long stiff cables

Earlier (NOTES §49.16) we documented that Method S's per-pair XPBD bend saturates around 5° on long stiff-bend cables regardless of K or bend_ke — caused by the locality limit of per-pair projection.

The fix: a second block-tridiagonal Thomas solve for bend, alongside the stretch Thomas. Bend has the same chain coupling structure as stretch, so the same Thomas trick applies. Implementation: ~200 lines (assembly + apply kernels; reuses existing vec3 Thomas solver via the isotropic-trace inertia simplification).

VBD + Thomas pre-pass (XPBD vs VBD architectural asymmetry)

Tested whether the Thomas locality fix can be retrofitted onto Newton's existing VBD. Added cable_thomas_solver opt-in to SolverVBD: per outer iter, run Method S's Thomas (stretch + optionally bend) BEFORE VBD's Gauss-Seidel sweep.

The naive pre-pass approach FAILS: Thomas projects positions to satisfy distance, then VBD's GS sweep includes the same stretch energy and tries to enforce again — the two solvers double-apply the constraint and energy explodes.

This empirically confirms the architectural asymmetry: XPBD's modular constraint kernels make Thomas drop in cleanly (Method S + Thomas-bend ships); VBD's unified-energy block solve needs kernel-level surgery to host an external solver. Proper VBD + Thomas integration would require modifying _solve_rigid_body_iteration to subtract Thomas-handled constraint terms from the per-body block — 3-5 days of careful work, not pursued here.

Conclusion: for cable simulation with locality-limited solver, the right architecture is SolverCable as a first-class XPBD-family solver (Method S + Thomas-bend), NOT "add Thomas to VBD."

Update 2026-06-19/20: revised verdict. VBD+Thomas CAN be made to work with 3 ingredients: (1) inertia-target rewrite after Thomas (the bridge), (2) PBD contact projection in pre-pass for contact-feasibility, (3) Thomas-bend for rotation stability. Stable on 12 of 14 scenes — chains + piles with non-trivial bend. Drift matches Method S. Stretch 2-5× worse. Fails on rope-like piles (very-soft / zero bend). Niche option for VBD-features + guaranteed-stiff cables; Method S remains the universal default.

VBD + SOR — tested as a potential fallback, NOT universal

Ported the Method S SOR trick to Newton's existing SolverVBD as a 1-line addition to its outer iter loop (kernel _sor_blend_body_q in rigid_vbd_kernels.py, conditional on solver.sor_omega). Initially looked promising on short tests; full 13-scene sweep revealed serious stability problems:

VBD+SOR is stable in only 5 of 13 scenes (piles with non-trivial bend). The SOR overshoot requires either stiff bend or strong contact dissipation to be absorbed; free chains have neither, and zero-bend piles fail too. VBD+SOR is NOT a universal fallback. Method S remains the only universally stable solver in our toolkit.

Implication for the "energy-conservation" use case: the genuine VBD energy advantage only holds at ω=1.0 (no SOR). With ω=1.5, VBD picks up the same dissipative coupling Method S uses anyway.

Engineering insights (NOTES §47): (1) The angular impulse and Schur correction must both be present — adding only the impulse without the diagonal correction destabilises the Thomas solve. (2) The bend kernel's frame transform is non-optional; it works on chains by accident (parents share orientation) and silently explodes on piles. (3) relax = 1.0 for the bend constraint is correct; lower values under-correct, higher values overshoot into zig-zag.

Method axes (judged per scene)

A method passes only if it clears all three — speed, residual/energy correctness, and visual physical plausibility. Any zig-zag, over-damping, pin drift, contact buzz, or energy gain disqualifies it regardless of residual.
